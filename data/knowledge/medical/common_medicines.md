TITLE: Common Medicines - Usage and Dosages

KEYWORDS: medication, dosage, paracetamol, acetaminophen, ibuprofen, ORS, antiseptic, pain relief, fever reduction

SEVERITY LEVELS:
- Safe use: Following proper dosages and guidelines
- Risky: Exceeding dosages, combining incorrectly, using contraindicated
- Dangerous: Overdose, wrong medication, giving to wrong person

WHEN TO USE THIS GUIDE:
- Person needs pain relief
- Person has fever
- Person has inflammation or swelling
- Person is dehydrated
- Wound needs cleaning
- Before giving any medication

IMMEDIATE DANGERS:
- Overdose can cause liver failure, kidney failure, or death
- Wrong medication can cause serious harm
- Combining medications incorrectly can be dangerous
- Giving to children or elderly requires different dosages
- Some medications are contraindicated in certain conditions

STEP-BY-STEP ACTIONS (DO THIS IN ORDER):

1. IDENTIFY THE PROBLEM:
   - What symptom needs treatment?
   - Is medication appropriate or can other methods work?
   - What is the underlying cause?

2. CHECK CONTRAINDICATIONS:
   - Does person have conditions that make medication unsafe?
   - Is person taking other medications?
   - Is person allergic to medication?
   - Is person pregnant or breastfeeding?
   - See specific medication sections below

3. CALCULATE CORRECT DOSAGE:
   - Use weight for children (most accurate)
   - Use age if weight unknown (less accurate)
   - Do NOT guess - better to give less than more
   - See dosage tables below

4. ADMINISTER SAFELY:
   - Give with food if stomach upset possible
   - Give with water
   - Do NOT crush tablets unless directed
   - Do NOT mix with alcohol

5. MONITOR FOR SIDE EFFECTS:
   - Watch for allergic reactions
   - Watch for worsening symptoms
   - Stop if serious side effects occur
   - MEDICATION INTERACTIONS (CRITICAL):
     * Do NOT combine paracetamol with other paracetamol-containing medicines
     * Do NOT combine ibuprofen with aspirin (increases bleeding risk)
     * Do NOT combine multiple pain medicines without knowing interactions
     * Alcohol increases risk with many medicines
     * Some medicines affect blood clotting - do not combine
   - OVERDOSE RECOGNITION:
     * Paracetamol overdose: Nausea, vomiting, abdominal pain, liver damage (may not show for 24-48 hours)
     * Ibuprofen overdose: Stomach pain, nausea, dizziness, kidney problems
     * If overdose suspected: Stop medication, monitor closely
     * Some overdoses may not show symptoms immediately
     * When in doubt: Give less, not more

PARACETAMOL (ACETAMINOPHEN):

USES:
- Reduces fever
- Reduces pain
- Safer for stomach than ibuprofen

DOSAGES (BY WEIGHT - MOST ACCURATE):
- Adults: 500-1000mg every 4-6 hours, maximum 4000mg per day
- Children (weight-based):
  * 10-15mg per kg body weight per dose
  * Every 4-6 hours
  * Maximum 4 doses per day
  * Maximum 60mg per kg per day

DOSAGES (BY AGE IF WEIGHT UNKNOWN):
- Adults: 500-1000mg every 4-6 hours
- 12-17 years: 500-1000mg every 4-6 hours
- 6-11 years: 250-500mg every 4-6 hours
- 2-5 years: 120-250mg every 4-6 hours
- Under 2 years: Consult weight-based dosing, do not guess

CONTRAINDICATIONS:
- Severe liver disease
- Already taking other paracetamol-containing medicines
- Allergy to paracetamol

WARNINGS:
- Overdose causes liver failure (can be fatal)
- Do NOT exceed maximum daily dose
- Do NOT combine with other paracetamol-containing medicines
- Alcohol increases liver damage risk

IBUPROFEN:

USES:
- Reduces pain
- Reduces inflammation and swelling
- Reduces fever

DOSAGES (BY WEIGHT - MOST ACCURATE):
- Adults: 200-400mg every 6-8 hours, maximum 1200mg per day
- Children (weight-based):
  * 5-10mg per kg body weight per dose
  * Every 6-8 hours
  * Maximum 3-4 doses per day
  * Maximum 40mg per kg per day

DOSAGES (BY AGE IF WEIGHT UNKNOWN):
- Adults: 200-400mg every 6-8 hours
- 12-17 years: 200-400mg every 6-8 hours
- 6-11 years: 100-200mg every 6-8 hours
- Under 6 years: Use weight-based dosing, do not guess

CONTRAINDICATIONS:
- Stomach ulcers or bleeding
- Severe kidney disease
- Severe heart failure
- Allergy to ibuprofen or aspirin
- Dehydration (increases kidney risk)
- Pregnancy (third trimester)

WARNINGS:
- Can cause stomach bleeding
- Can cause kidney damage, especially if dehydrated
- Do NOT give if person is dehydrated
- Do NOT combine with aspirin (increases bleeding risk)

ASPIRIN:

USES:
- Reduces pain and fever
- Reduces inflammation
- Blood thinning (in heart attack - advanced use)

DOSAGES:
- Adults: 325-650mg every 4-6 hours, maximum 4000mg per day
- Children: Generally AVOID (Reye's syndrome risk)
- Do NOT give to children under 18 unless specifically directed

CONTRAINDICATIONS:
- Children under 18 (Reye's syndrome risk)
- Bleeding disorders
- Stomach ulcers
- Allergy to aspirin
- Pregnancy (can cause bleeding)

WARNINGS:
- Can cause stomach bleeding
- Can cause Reye's syndrome in children (fatal)
- Do NOT give to children
- Do NOT combine with other blood thinners

ORAL REHYDRATION SOLUTION (ORS):

USES:
- Prevents and treats dehydration
- Life-saving for diarrhea and vomiting
- Replaces electrolytes lost through illness

PREPARATION:
- If packets available: Follow packet instructions
- Homemade: 1 liter clean water + 6 teaspoons sugar + 1/2 teaspoon salt
- Mix until dissolved
- Use within 24 hours

DOSAGES:
- Give after every loose stool or vomiting episode
- Small sips frequently (every 5-10 minutes)
- Continue until person urinates normally
- No maximum - give as needed

CONTRAINDICATIONS:
- None for oral use
- Do NOT give if person cannot swallow (choking risk)

ANTISEPTICS:

USES:
- Clean skin around wounds
- Reduce infection risk

COMMON TYPES:
- Alcohol (70% isopropyl or ethanol)
- Iodine (povidone-iodine)
- Hydrogen peroxide (3%)
- Chlorhexidine (if available)

USAGE:
- Apply to skin AROUND wound, not deep inside
- Clean wound with water first
- Apply antiseptic to surrounding skin
- Do NOT pour deep into wounds (damages tissue)

CONTRAINDICATIONS:
- Allergy to specific antiseptic
- Do NOT use in eyes
- Do NOT use on large burns (can be absorbed)

WHAT NOT TO DO:
- DO NOT exceed maximum dosages
- DO NOT combine multiple pain medicines without knowing interactions
- DO NOT give adult dosages to children
- DO NOT guess dosages - better to give less
- DO NOT use unknown medicines
- DO NOT give medicines to unconscious person (choking risk)
- DO NOT combine paracetamol with other paracetamol-containing medicines
- DO NOT give aspirin to children
- DO NOT give ibuprofen if dehydrated

IMPROVISED / LOW-RESOURCE OPTIONS:

NO MEASURING TOOLS:
- Use known volumes (teaspoon = 5ml approximately)
- Better to give less than more
- Do NOT guess - errors can be fatal

NO SPECIFIC MEDICINE:
- Focus on other treatments (rest, hydration, positioning)
- Some conditions do not require medication
- Natural methods may help (cooling for fever, etc.)

WHEN YOU MUST SEEK PROFESSIONAL HELP:
- Overdose suspected
- Serious side effects
- Medication not helping
- Person requires prescription medication
- Complex medical conditions
- Pregnancy or breastfeeding concerns

REALISTIC OUTCOMES & LIMITATIONS:

WITH PROPER USE:
- Medications can help with symptoms
- Proper dosages are generally safe
- Can improve comfort and recovery

WITHOUT PROFESSIONAL HELP:
- Some conditions require prescription medications (not available)
- Dosage errors can cause harm
- Some medications may not be available
- Focus on what is available and safe

SAFETY & ACCURACY RULES:

MEDICAL REASONING:
- Weight-based dosing is most accurate for children
- Maximum dosages prevent overdose
- Contraindications prevent serious harm
- Monitoring helps catch problems early

CLEAN VS DIRTY VS LAST-RESORT:
- Clean: Proper medications, accurate dosages, measuring tools (ideal)
- Dirty: Available medications, estimated dosages (acceptable if careful)
- Last-resort: Focus on non-medication treatments if medications unavailable or unsafe

MYTHS CORRECTED:
- "More is better" - FALSE: Overdose is dangerous
- "Natural means safe" - FALSE: Natural substances can be toxic
- "Children are small adults" - FALSE: Children need different dosages

STORAGE:
- Keep medicines in cool, dry place
- Keep out of reach of children
- Check expiration dates
- Do not use if expired or unknown
- STORAGE CONDITIONS:
  * Most medicines: Room temperature, dry place
  * Protect from light and moisture
  * Do not store in bathroom (humidity)
  * Do not store in car (heat)
  * Keep in original container if possible
  * Label clearly if removed from original container
  * Check for signs of deterioration (color change, crumbling)
- EXPIRATION:
  * Expired medicines may be less effective
  * Some expired medicines can be harmful
  * When in doubt: Do not use
  * In survival situation: May need to use expired medicines if nothing else available
  * Use with extra caution if expired
